Managing diabetes

The Control Factor

Food choices and exercise are the two things you can personally control. Understanding why they matter makes them easier to commit to.

🥦 Healthy Eating

The first and most powerful step to controlling diabetes

Since carbohydrates play a significant role in a diabetic nutritional plan, this cookbook gives you recipes organised so you can easily find what fits your daily plan. When you have an eating programme, creating balance becomes much easier.

Key guidelines to follow:
  • Regulating blood glucose is the single most important goal — always.
  • Eat fresh, unprocessed foods as much as possible — seasonal fruit and vegetables are ideal.
  • Develop an eating programme to ensure a balanced, consistent diet.
  • What you eat, how much you eat, and when you eat are all equally important.
  • Eating at the same time every day is very helpful for blood glucose management.
  • Know proper portions and stick to them consistently.
  • Read food labels — it becomes second nature quickly and makes a real difference.

🏃 Exercise

Essential alongside healthy eating — not optional

Exercise not only makes you feel better mentally, it promotes better functioning of your body's systems. Diabetics need this boost to help avoid — or repair — damage due to diabetes.

Key benefits of regular movement:
  • Helps to lower blood glucose levels directly and naturally.
  • Helps lower or maintain a healthy weight.
  • Improves circulation throughout the body.
  • Lowers stress, which directly affects blood glucose.

Getting started

Creating Your Plan

The four pillars of a successful diabetes management plan. Simple to understand, powerful when applied consistently.

1

Sample Your Goals

Set reasonable, achievable goals and build from there. Small consistent wins lead to big long-term results. Don't try to change everything overnight.

2

Keep a Journal

Track what you eat, how you feel, and your blood glucose readings. Seeing your progress on paper is one of the most powerful motivators there is.

3

Find Your Support

Find a friend to exercise with, join a class, or work with a healthcare coach. Having accountability makes every step easier and more sustainable.

4

Stay Focused

Keep the big picture in mind — controlling diabetes so you feel better, live longer, and enjoy more of life. Every good meal choice is a step toward that.

Important: Always talk with your healthcare practitioner before beginning any new exercise regimen or making significant changes to your diet. They can help you determine which approaches are safest for your specific situation.

Education

Understanding Diabetes

To make effective changes in your diet and lifestyle, it helps to understand what you are managing and why these changes are so important.

Type 1 Diabetes

  • Formerly referred to as juvenile diabetes or insulin-dependent diabetes.
  • Type 1 is an autoimmune disorder. The immune system attacks and destroys the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas.
  • A genetic disposition, virus, infection, or other trigger is thought to initiate the immune response.
  • A family history of diabetes is a primary indicator of the likelihood of having Type 1.

Type 2 Diabetes

  • Formerly referred to as adult-onset diabetes.
  • Insulin resistance is the main cause of Type 2 diabetes.
  • Potential causes of insulin resistance: obesity, genetic disposition, family history.
  • Risk factors include being 40+, certain ethnic backgrounds, high blood pressure, high cholesterol, history of gestational diabetes.

Potential Risk Factors for Type 2 Diabetes

  • Being 40 years of age or older.
  • Being of Hispanic, Native American, South Asian, Asian, or African descent.
  • Having high blood pressure and/or high cholesterol.
  • Having a history of gestational diabetes.
  • Obesity or being significantly overweight.

Why it matters

Consequences of Poorly Controlled Diabetes

Controlling your blood glucose is imperative to your body's proper functioning. Understanding the risks makes the daily effort worthwhile.

Mild — But Reversible

These symptoms often improve with better blood sugar control.

  • Blurred vision
  • Fatigue and low energy
  • Frequent urination and thirst
  • Gum and urinary tract infections
  • Obesity / weight difficulties
  • Slow healing of wounds and skin irritation

Short-Term Complications

These require swift medical attention if they occur.

  • Ketoacidosis
  • Buildup of ketone bodies and acidity in the blood
  • Occurs when the body does not produce enough insulin
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic Syndrome (HHNS)
  • Blood glucose spikes dramatically — a medical emergency
  • May result in coma or seizures if untreated
  • Hypoglycemia (low blood glucose)
  • Sweating, rapid heartbeat, hunger, nervousness, confusion

Long-Term Complications

Occur when diabetes is poorly managed over many years.

  • Diabetic Nephropathy
  • Kidney damage that may lead to kidney failure
  • Diabetic Retinopathy
  • Damage to the retina — can lead to blindness if untreated
  • Diabetic Neuropathy
  • Nerve damage causing tingling, numbness in the feet
  • Cardiovascular Disease
  • Blockages that increase risk of heart attack or stroke

Other Complications

Additional effects that can develop over time.

  • Eyes
  • Cataracts and glaucoma risk increases
  • Mental Health
  • High glucose may affect dopamine regulation, increasing risk of depression and anxiety
  • Hearing
  • Loss of hearing due to auditory nerve damage
  • Gastroparesis
  • Stomach muscles stop functioning properly due to nerve damage
